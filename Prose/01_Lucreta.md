#Prologue

##Lucreta

She sits quietly on the corner of her bed. The room dims with passing clouds through the steel framed window. Vera arranges the headdress of white orchids and rosemary, carefully weaving them into her crimson hair.

Her fingers trace the ornate mahogany at the foot of the bed, following the intricate curves carved into the wood from where they began to where they ended. Her hand stops. Her ear turns toward the closed window. Beyond it, carriage wheels roll across stone. Voices follow.

She moves her hand to her waist. Standing, she gently pushes Vera aside. Her stride stiffens, but it is barely noticed. Vera speaks no words, instead dropping her head as Lucreta passes by.

The outside brims with life. Carriages rolling across cobbled streets. The suits and dresses of passing lives. Wooden caskets stacked with bottles. Baskets overflowing with fruit. The parasols. The hats. The bags. The canes. Lucreta keeps looking as Vera gently pulls her away from the window.

The street slips from view. Her eyes lift. Above the rooftops, a bright blue sky. She closes her eyes.

A gentle knock stirred the silence, though the voice that followed carried far less patience.

“Lucreta! Are you ready yet? The guests have been waiting nearly an hour already. Surely you do not intend to leave your groom languishing from boredom before he can even marry you.”

When she opened them again, her pale-mint eyes anchored her reflection, erasing everything else. Slowly, the rest of her came into focus around them—the ivory folds of her wedding gown blooming toward the floor, lace contours contrasting the vivid colors her eyes had lost.

Vera's gaze lingered upon the reflection for only a moment before falling away. Her hands returned to the delicate strands cascading over Lucreta's shoulders.

“Do not tell me you have grown fearful of womanhood at the final hour, dear. You linger so long one might think this marriage burdens you terribly.”

The room settled into silence.

Lucreta's gaze remained fixed.

“No, Vera. Quite the contrary.”

A faint smile touched her lips.

“Nothing could—”

The words broke apart beneath a sudden spasm in her chest. She turned sharply, one hand catching the edge of the table as the cough tore free from her throat.

For a moment she could not breathe.

When it passed, she drew in a slow, careful breath.

“Nothing could mar this day,” she said quietly. “There is nothing I have ever desired more than this hour.”

The cough returned before the final word had fully left her mouth. Sharper this time. A faint stain bloomed against her lips. Her eyes widened. A hand rose quickly to her mouth. She wiped the blood away before turning her palm against the fabric of her gown, as though it might disappear there.

Only then remembering what she was wearing.

Vera's hands stilled.

For the briefest moment, worry surfaced across her reflection. Then something practiced settled over it instead. A careful smile. Gentle enough to preserve the moment rather than shatter it.

“So—”

A brush slid through her hair.

“How long before I may tend to the child you are certain to bless us with?”

A subtle tilt of Lucreta's chin.

“And yet you speak of that child so readily.”

A glance toward Vera.

“As though you have spent longer preparing for its coming than my own mother ever spent preparing for me.”

Vera's smile weakened briefly, taking much of the room's fragile warmth with it.

Lucreta watched her.

A knock burst through the moment.

“Lucreta! Your father will grow tired of the groom before you are even married. Come out, girl—at this rate you shall emerge old and wrinkled before the ceremony begins.”

Vera slid a hand across Lucreta's shoulder and down her arm before stepping away.

“Go, dear. No one deserves this moment more than you.”

A tear slipped down her cheek. She wiped it away before it could fall any further. Turning slightly, she hid her reflection from view.

Lucreta straightened slowly and gathered the skirts of her gown with practiced grace.

A blur halted her step.

Again.

She continued toward the door. From the corner of her eye, a faint trail of her reflection lingered a fraction too long.

As though it too had faltered.

Her hand closed around the handle.

Silence.

“L—ta!”

Her fingers loosened. Sliding along polished brass.

“—creta!”

The room lurched sideways. A violent knock tore through everything else, swallowing her senses in a single sharp impact of sound.

Her pale-mint eyes remained fixed and hollow as her body crumpled across the floor.
