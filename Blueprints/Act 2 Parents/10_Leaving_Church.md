# 10 — Leaving Church

## Status

- Scene blueprint
- Prose exists
- Adaptation/script version not written

## Act

- Act 02 — Parents
- Part 01 — Marriage To Lake

## Characters

- Philip / Phil
- Abigail / Abby

## Location

- Cheap Las Vegas-like wedding chapel
- Chapel doorway
- Futuristic populated city outside

## Purpose

- Open the Parents act through movement, threshold, and world reveal.
- Introduce Philip and Abigail through sound and motion before identity.
- Let the chapel’s artificial celebration physically follow them into the next scene through confetti.

## Scene Summary

The scene begins in darkness behind a chapel bench, with the camera low at ground level. One footstep is heard before Philip and Abigail are seen. Their feet pass through the shot first. After they pass the bench, the camera catches them already moving and begins following from behind. It starts low on their feet and gradually rises, picking up their full bodies from behind as they approach the doorway.

The chapel is revealed as a cheap imitation of sacredness: colored reflections sliding over worn floorboards, hidden lights behind painted glass, plastic wood-like walls, and forced ceremonial warmth. The space borrows the visual language of a church, but the materials betray it as commercial and artificial.

As they near the door, confetti suddenly explodes onto them and fake applause plays from speakers. The celebration is automated, tacky, and unexpected. Philip and Abigail are startled, then look at each other. Abby starts removing confetti from herself right away, while Phil opens the door and puts on his sunglasses before brushing the confetti off himself.

The outside world is revealed before their faces. For two or three seconds, the viewer sees and hears the futuristic populated city: noise, movement, artificial light, life, and pressure. The fake chapel celebration still clings to them as they enter the harsher real world outside. Only after the world has entered the scene does the view shift to the front and reveal Philip and Abigail’s faces.

## Emotional Function

- Start emotion: quiet transition after Lucreta’s heavier wedding imagery.
- Shift: fake sacredness gives way to cheap commercial celebration, then public city life.
- End emotion: Phil and Abby step into the world with different reactions to the chapel’s fake celebration.
- Viewer should feel: a doorway beginning rather than a doorway ending.

## Character Function

- Philip and Abigail are introduced first through shared movement, not explanation.
- Their relationship is framed as forward motion.
- Their first human reaction is shared surprise at the fake celebration.
- Their faces are withheld until the viewer has felt the world they are entering.

## Structural Function

- Moves from visual threshold into world reveal.
- Carries the fake chapel celebration into the street through confetti still stuck to them.
- Sets up Scene 20, where dialogue and relationship rhythm become the main engine.

## Visual / Camera Function

- Start hidden behind a chapel bench at ground level.
- Introduce the couple through sound and movement before identity.
- Start with their feet in shot as they pass the bench.
- After they pass, the camera begins following them and slowly rises to pick up the whole body from behind.
- By the time they near the doorway, the frame should hold their backs, not their faces.
- Reveal the chapel as a cheap imitation of sacredness without mocking Philip or Abigail.
- Let the fake celebration system surprise them before they leave.
- When the doors open, let the outside world enter before the face reveal.
- Shift to the front only after the viewer has absorbed the city.

## Timing / Pacing

- The opening should feel like the camera catches the couple already moving, not like a staged entrance.
- The confetti/fake applause beat should be sudden enough to startle them.
- The city-first reveal should be brief but deliberate: around two or three seconds.
- Do not rush immediately to the faces.
- Do not hold so long that the city steals the scene.
- The order matters: movement first, cheap ritual second, world third, faces fourth.

## Dialogue / Cue Attachments

### F01

- Cue:
  - Start from behind a chapel bench.
  - Camera is low at ground level.
  - The frame begins in black or near-black, blocked by the bench.

### F02

- Cue:
  - We hear one footstep before clearly seeing them.
  - The sound introduces movement before identity.

### F03

- Cue:
  - Their feet are the first visible part of them.
  - As they leave the cover of the bench, they are already passing by.
  - The camera does not wait for them to enter like a staged reveal.

### F04

- Cue:
  - The camera catches them mid-motion and begins to follow.
  - It should still be low, close to feet and lower legs.

### F05

- Cue:
  - After they pass, the camera slowly starts going up.
  - The rise should gradually pick up the whole body from behind.
  - This keeps the feet-first opening while preparing the back-facing doorway image.

### F06

- Cue:
  - Colored reflections move across the worn floorboards, but they should not feel holy.
  - The colors come from cheap painted glass and hidden lights, creating a Las Vegas-like imitation of religious beauty.
  - Use fake lights, plastic wood-like walls, worn floorboards, painted glass, and forced warmth.
  - The chapel is cheap, but the couple should not feel cheap.

### F07

- Cue:
  - By the doorway, the camera has risen enough to see their backs clearly.
  - Their faces remain withheld.
  - The image should connect to wedding-threshold language while still feeling mobile and alive.

### F08

- Cue:
  - The confetti should feel sudden and tacky.
  - It should surprise them, not feel elegant.
  - Confetti should stick to hair, shoulders, and clothes.

### F09

- Cue:
  - The applause is not from people.
  - It comes from speakers.
  - The sound should feel canned, artificial, and slightly too cheerful for the mostly empty chapel.

### F10

- Cue:
  - They are startled by it.
  - They look at each other after the surprise lands.
  - The reaction should be funny: a flinch, sudden blink, awkward half-laugh, or shared look.
  - The fake celebration should not mock their marriage; it mocks the cheap commercial shell around it.

### F11

- Cue:
  - Abby starts removing confetti from herself immediately.
  - Her reaction is practical and instinctive.
  - This gives Abby a different rhythm from Phil: she cleans herself up while he stays in doorway/gesture mode.

### F12

- Cue:
  - Phil opens the door after the startled shared look.
  - The doorway remains a beginning, not a pause.
  - He does not immediately remove the confetti from himself.

### F13

- Cue:
  - Phil puts on his sunglasses before brushing off the confetti.
  - This makes his later cool-guy performance funnier because the fake chapel celebration is still stuck to him.
  - He prioritizes the image first, then deals with the mess.

### F14

- Cue:
  - Their first step outside carries the human aftermath of the fake celebration.
  - Abby is already removing confetti.
  - Phil still has confetti on him and only begins brushing it off after the door/shades beat.
  - This creates a physical bridge into Scene 20.

### F15

- Cue:
  - The city appears before the faces.
  - The viewer should feel the world opening before meeting the couple emotionally.

### F16

- Cue:
  - Hold only two or three seconds.
  - The city should feel populated, loud, futuristic, and alive.
  - The city’s harsh reality should contrast with the chapel’s fake celebration.

### F17

- Cue:
  - Reveal their faces only after the world has entered the scene.
  - Once their faces are revealed, the focus can shift to them.

## Important Notes

- This scene should not become a dialogue scene.
- Its job is threshold, movement, world reveal, and the fake commercial wedding shell.
- The emotional value is not in the chapel; it is in Philip and Abigail walking forward together.
- Confetti should carry into the next scene, giving Phil something to brush off while performing confidence.
- Abby should begin removing confetti immediately; Phil delays because he opens the door and puts on the sunglasses first.
- Once the faces are revealed, the next scene can begin the dialogue rhythm without awkwardly breaking it later.
