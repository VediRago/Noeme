# 20 — Walk To Street Crossing

## Location

- Compressed street life outside the old chapel

## Purpose

- Introduce Phil and Abby’s relationship through dialogue, movement, and physical rhythm.
- Let the audience hear their voices after Scene 10 introduces the world and their faces.
- Show their wedding happiness moving directly through the pressure of the city.
- Carry the fake chapel celebration into the real street through confetti still clinging to them.
- End as they approach the group waiting to cross, before the beggar / necklace event takes focus.

## Scene Summary

Philip and Abigail move away from the chapel and into the compressed street life outside. The fake wedding glow still clings to them in bits of confetti as the real city presses in around them: crowded stalls, food smells, traffic noise, bodies brushing past, steam, cheap light, poverty, exhaustion, horns, and overlapping voices.

Phil begins the relationship rhythm with a joke: “You know you have to deal with me for the rest of your life now, right?” As he says it, he reaches for his sunglasses, preparing his exaggerated cool-guy performance while still carrying pieces of the chapel’s fake celebration. Abby answers by cutting through it, treating the marriage as a mutual trap rather than a romantic fantasy.

As they walk, the city keeps interrupting them. A flower merchant calls out. Phil gives only a quick glance back to refuse, keeps walking, then anticipates the flower pitch himself and turns it into a joke for Abby. He is trying to stay smooth — sunglasses, confetti, confidence, performance — when his foot catches a bottle beside a homeless man resting against the wall. Abby looks back toward the church and asks if it is too late to go back.

A child bumps into Abby and disappears into the crowd. Phil lowers his sunglasses, winks, and jokes about karma. Abby calls him insufferable. He accepts the title like a badge.

The scene continues as Abby hooks her arm through his and turns the joke toward the reality of their life together: the wreck he calls home must become livable. Phil slows, realizing he may be the one being tricked. Abby keeps walking. When she turns back with wind pulling hair across her face, her smile comes and goes before she moves on. The city compresses around them again, and the scene ends as they approach a group of people waiting to cross the street.

## Emotional Function

- Start emotion: newlywed playfulness entering a harsh public world.
- Shift: the city keeps pressing into their rhythm through noise, poverty, interruption, and crowd movement.
- End emotion: Abby leads the rhythm forward, teasing Phil while quietly grounding the marriage in real life.
- Viewer should feel: warmth surviving inside pressure.

## Character Function

### Phil

- Introduced through line plus gesture.
- Playful, theatrical, self-aware, trying to look smooth.
- Uses sunglasses as part of his joking self-presentation.
- His confidence is funny because Abby and the city can puncture it instantly.

### Abby

- Introduced as sharp, affectionate, and completely unfooled by Phil’s performance.
- Moves forward even when he slows.
- Shows warmth through teasing, not sentimental declaration.
- Begins grounding the marriage in practical reality: home, living conditions, future.

## Structural Function

- Moves from visual introduction into voice and relationship rhythm.
- Establishes Parents as warmer and more conversational than Lucreta, without making the world soft.
- Carries the fake chapel celebration into the harsh street through confetti still stuck to them.
- Prepares the beggar / necklace scene by letting poverty, crowd pressure, and traffic noise enter before the moral encounter becomes central.

## World / Scene Texture

- The corridor feeling is part of the city’s visual pressure, not necessarily the literal path they walk through the whole time.
- People, stalls, walls, signs, vehicles, and metal infrastructure compress around them, forming narrow passages before the flow opens again without stopping.
- Keep the street specific to this walk: crowded stalls, food smells, vendor calls, bodies brushing past, steam, fog, cheap lights, scattered bottles, traffic noise, horns, and people talking.
- The world should press into the couple without stopping them.
- Poverty and exhaustion are present as part of the street’s normal rhythm, not as a dramatic reveal.
- The couple’s wedding clothes and remaining confetti should briefly stand out, but the city should not stop for them.

## Visual / Camera Function

- Keep the couple moving; do not freeze the scene into exposition.
- Let noise compete with Phil and Abby’s dialogue without fully taking the scene away from them.
- The camera should keep returning to their rhythm: faces, hands, arm-hooking, sunglasses, glances, near-stumbles, brushing confetti, and small reactions.
- End as they approach a waiting crowd at the street crossing, before the act of crossing becomes the next scene’s focus.

## Dialogue / Cue Attachments

### F02

- Dialogue:
  - Phil: “You know you have to deal with me for the rest of your life now, right?”
- Cue:
  - Phil reaches for his sunglasses as he says it.
  - The sunglasses are his playful “cool guy” mask, not real arrogance.
  - Bits of confetti may still be on him from the chapel.
  - The cool-guy pose is already slightly undercut by the fake celebration clinging to him.

### F03

- Dialogue:
  - Abby: “I wasn’t aware this was a one-sided deal. Are you saying I’ve been duped?”
- Cue:
  - Abby answers while walking.
  - She feels used to this routine.
  - Her affection is in how quickly she cuts through him.

### F04–F05

- Dialogue:
  - Merchant: “A gift for the missus?”
  - Phil: “No need.”
- Cue:
  - Phil gives only a quick glance back, keeps walking, and replies.
  - The city interrupts them, but does not stop them.
  - His main focus remains on the walk, Abby, the sunglasses performance, and the remaining confetti.

### F06

- Dialogue:
  - Phil: “Oh, and the flowers?”
  - Phil: “She’s already got me, thank you.”
- Cue:
  - Phil knows the obvious flower sale before it is made.
  - He does not need to turn back again.
  - He turns the interruption into part of his performance for Abby.
  - His divided attention sets up the stumble immediately after.

### F07

- Cue:
  - Right after the cool line, Phil turns forward and catches his foot on one of the bottles.
  - The stumble should puncture the cool-guy act instantly.
  - Physical comedy and city misery occupy the same frame.
  - The homeless man should not be framed as a dramatic reveal; he is part of normalized hardship.

### F08

- Dialogue:
  - Abby: “Is it too late to go back?”
- Cue:
  - Abby looks back toward the church.
  - The joke is physically tied to the doorway they just left.

### F09

- Dialogue:
  - Phil: “You just watched me almost fall within a few steps and already want to go back? Am I supposed to survive this day?”
- Cue:
  - He recovers verbally faster than physically.
  - His embarrassment becomes more material for the joke.

### F10

- Cue:
  - The child cutting through the crowd shows the city’s lack of personal space.
  - The interruption is normal city movement, not a plot event.

### F11

- Dialogue:
  - Phil: “Karma here strikes fast, don’t you think?”
- Cue:
  - Phil lowers his sunglasses.
  - The sunglasses become comic punctuation again.

### F12

- Cue:
  - The wink should feel knowingly ridiculous.
  - He is rebuilding “cool guy mode” after the city has already undercut him.

### F13

- Dialogue:
  - Abby: “Are you always this insufferable, darling?”
- Cue:
  - “Darling” should carry affection and mockery at the same time.
  - She is not annoyed in a cold way; she enjoys puncturing him.

### F14

- Dialogue:
  - Phil: “It’s my middle name. My legacy. My most honed skill.”
- Cue:
  - Phil places hand to chest / chin up.
  - He accepts the insult as if it were an honor.
  - The delivery should be proudly absurd.

### F15

- Cue:
  - Abby hooking her arm through his proves the affection beneath the teasing.
  - The touch should not be overplayed.

### F16

- Dialogue:
  - Abby: “The skills you ought to be focused on right now are the ones that’ll make that wreck you call home actually livable.”
- Cue:
  - This line pivots the scene from wedding performance into actual life together.
  - It should still feel playful, not bitter.

### F17–F18

- Dialogue:
  - Phil: “Am I being duped?”
- Cue:
  - Phil slows.
  - The pressure of the joke reverses back onto him.
  - Abby leading forward matters.

### F19

- Dialogue:
  - Abby: “Maybe.”
- Cue:
  - This should be a small, memorable image.
  - Her smile should be brief, not held too long.

### F20

- Cue:
  - Abby does not wait for Phil to catch up emotionally.
  - She keeps the marriage moving forward.

### F21

- Cue:
  - The corridor-like feeling comes from pressure around them, not from one fixed corridor location.
  - People and metal close around the couple for a moment, forcing the movement into a narrow passage.
  - The flow opens again without stopping, keeping the scene in motion.

### F22

- Cue:
  - End on approach, not crossing.
  - They come toward a group of people waiting at the street crossing.
  - Busy traffic, horns, and overlapping voices should already be audible, preparing the next scene.

## Important Notes

- This scene should not romanticize poverty, but it also should not turn poverty into a dramatic spectacle.
- Phil and Abby’s warmth matters more because it is not protected from the world.
- The sunglasses gesture is essential to Phil’s introduction.
- The confetti from Scene 10 should physically carry into this scene and help undercut Phil’s cool-guy act.
- The bottle stumble should feel motivated by divided attention: walking, performing, answering the merchant, sunglasses, and brushing off the fake chapel celebration.
- Abby’s “Maybe” is a clean ending button because she keeps moving forward and leaves Phil reacting.
- The beggar request and necklace gift should not be handled here; they belong to the next scene.
