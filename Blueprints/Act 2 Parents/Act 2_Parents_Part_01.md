Part Summary:
Philip and Abigail leave a cheap wedding chapel just after marrying. The chapel imitates sacredness through colored glass, fake warmth, automated confetti, and canned applause, but the emotional truth belongs to the couple, not the ceremony. Their first movement is forward: out through the doorway and into the city.

The city greets them with pressure. Sirens, horns, stalls, bodies, smells, traffic, fog, and compressed concrete close around them. Phil performs confidence through sunglasses and jokes. Abby answers him with equal sharpness. Their banter becomes a way of staying together inside a world that keeps interrupting them.

A beggar interrupts the rhythm. Phil has nothing to give, exposing their own limits, but Abby gives her necklace. The act is not staged as sainthood. She immediately folds the moment back into humor, keeping the scene alive instead of sentimental. The city does not stop to honor the gesture. Bodies and metal compress around them, pushing them toward the car.

On the way to the car, Abby is pulled toward a sharp cry in the crowd. Phil catches her arm and redirects her without scolding her. He uses humor to pull her back into their rhythm. The car becomes a temporary pocket of privacy, but not true safety. Once the doors close, the street noise drops to a muffle and the couple gets their first small shelter from the city.

Inside the car, the wedding energy begins to fade. Abby settles against the window and drifts into reflection. Thousands of people move beyond the glass without looking at her or at each other. Phil notices her slipping away and checks on her gently. Abby asks where they go from here, meaning more than the route. Phil answers the practical version and gives her room. Fog presses against every window. Abby says they are not even privileged enough to see the sky. Silence takes control.

The city interrupts even that silence through ordinary traffic. The brake jolt pulls Abby back into the immediate world. There is no special accident or plot event. The city itself is the pressure.

Afterward, the part takes a breath. Abby sleeps while Phil drives. The city gradually thins, daylight softens, and night comes without fully completing itself because city glow still bleeds into the distance. This passage exists as rest, geography, and pacing. It lets the audience feel that time has truly passed.

Abby wakes hungry, and the couple's rhythm returns. Hunger interrupts reflection in a human way. Phil jokes that she has been asleep for two days. Abby complains, threatens to become his nightmare if he does not find food, and turns the joke back on him. Their banter restores warmth after the quiet.

Night takes them along unnamed roads outside the city. Abby distracts herself with her phone until Phil makes her notice what she has missed. The headlights eat the final stretch of dirt road, and beyond it the world opens. Abby sees vastness for the first time in the part. The city had left no room for anything beyond itself. She is struck quiet.

The car stops. Phil turns off the engine and the lights, letting the universe move in around them. He asks Abby to cover her eyes, breaks the tension with another stupid joke, then reveals a key in his palm. Abby does not know what it opens yet, but she understands that it matters. Phil tells her this is their freedom. Then he gives the final truth: this is their new home.

Relationship To Lucreta:
- Lucreta is stopped before reaching the wedding threshold.
- Philip and Abigail begin by crossing through theirs.
- Lucreta's wedding is beautiful, formal, controlled, and fatal.
- Parents begins with a cheap, artificial wedding shell, but the relationship inside it is alive.
- Lucreta's doorway fails.
- Phil and Abby's doorway opens.
- This part should feel like the living inversion of Lucreta, while still carrying pressure, poverty, fog, and uncertainty.

World / Design Language:
- The chapel is fake sacredness: cheap lights, painted glass, plastic wood, canned applause, automated confetti.
- The city is compressed, loud, fogged, crowded, and indifferent.
- The car is temporary shelter, not real escape.
- The road toward the lake should gradually expand the world.
- The lake is the first true open space of the act.
- The final reveal should feel intimate, not grandiose.

Phil / Abby Dynamic:
- Phil uses jokes, performance, and mock confidence to keep movement alive.
- Abby answers him sharply and affectionately.
- Their banter is not filler; it is their survival language.
- Phil is not oblivious. He notices Abby's mood and gives her space.
- Abby is not fragile. She is sensitive to the city's pressure but remains active, funny, generous, and difficult in the best way.
