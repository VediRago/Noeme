# NOEME — Pilot Bible

**Original story concept developed:** 20 May 2026

---

## SERIES OVERVIEW

**Noeme** is an animated dramatic series built around a single hidden truth: across vastly different worlds, times, and lives, one underlying self keeps fragmenting into separate people. They do not share memory. They do not know each other. They simply — and fatally — share a pattern.

**The Condition** takes their eye color first. Then it takes everything else.

The pilot episode introduces this world through three movements:
1. A teaser that encodes the show's entire premise in a title sequence
2. The story of **Lucreta**, a woman in a Victorian-inspired world who cannot reach her own wedding
3. The story of **Philip and Abigail**, newlyweds in a compressed futuristic city who are leaving everything behind

One is denied the future. One proves the future exists. The audience will not understand why — yet.

### Format
Animated series. Episodes run approximately 44 minutes. The pilot may function as a feature-length entry (88 min) covering Acts 01 and 02 in full, or be split across two episodes.

### Tone
Quiet dread beneath warmth. The world does not announce its horror. Symptoms look biological. Tragedy looks like bad luck. The deeper architecture remains invisible until it cannot.

Visually: each act inhabits a fully distinct world. Lucreta's world is dim, aristocratic, ceremonially controlled. Parents' world is compressed, fogged, and futuristic. The audience should feel the difference before they understand the connection.

---

## PILOT EPISODE — "NOEME"

### Episode Structure

**TEASER** — The title sequence as premise. NOEME / PROTOTYPE dissolves into ONE ME. (~90 seconds, no dialogue)

**ACT 01** — LUCRETA. A failed threshold. A woman prepared for a life she cannot enter. (~18 minutes)

**ACT 02** — PARENTS. An arrived threshold. A couple leaving a fake chapel and driving toward a new life. (~24 minutes)

The audience does not receive an explanation connecting these three movements. The connection is felt through recurring images: doors, windows, sky, eye color, red hair, thresholds crossed and thresholds refused.

---

## TEASER

**Duration:** ~20 seconds. No dialogue. No narration.

The teaser is the show's premise disguised as a title card. It teaches the audience what FRAGMENTS is about before they know they are being taught.

Letters appear blurred at the edge of visibility: **NOEME** and, beneath it, **PROTOTYPE**. Barely readable. Layered underneath: two faces — dark hair, red hair, eyes that catch light. As the image resolves, music arrives simultaneously.

PROTOTYPE begins disappearing first. Letters vanish from its outermost positions. Each paired disappearance lights a letter in NOEME, gradually spelling **ONE ME** backwards until only the left O remains.

As the **ONE ME** reading becomes visible, one of the eyes turns pale. The Condition flickers into the title sequence before the audience has a name for what they are seeing.

The final O from PROTOTYPE falls into the O of NOEME. Once the two O letters meet, NOEME begins disappearing letter by letter, now spelling **ONE ME** forward.

The audience has just been told everything. They don't know it yet.

## ACT 01 — LUCRETA

**Duration:** ~18 minutes

**World:** Victorian-inspired. Not literal historical England — the visual language of ceremony, confinement, aristocratic ritual, and social control. Dark wood, polished brass, heavy fabric, formal gowns, mirrors. The room is dim, shadowed, beautiful, and controlled. The world outside the window is public, sunny, ordinary, and alive. The contrast is stark: Lucreta is inside ceremony while life happens without her.

**What Happens:** Lucreta is being prepared for a wedding inside a dim aristocratic room while ordinary civilian life continues outside the window. The act withholds her face initially, letting the audience discover her through posture, hands, ritual, attention, and physical failure. As she prepares, her body begins to betray her — a cough, blood at her lips, a stumble. When she reaches for the doorway to the ceremony, she collapses before the threshold.

**What It Teaches:** This act shows the audience how to read the show. Hands matter. Reflections matter. Windows matter. Sky matters. Small bodily failures carry as much meaning as anything spoken. No one explains the eye color. No one names the Condition. The audience receives evidence, not exposition.

---

## ACT 02 — PARENTS

**Duration:** ~24 minutes

**World:** Futuristic city. Dense, fogged, artificially lit. The sky is permanently absent — covered by fog so constant that no one alive has ever seen the city without it. Buildings compress, traffic locks, people move in all directions without making eye contact. Everything the act builds toward — the lake, the sky, the vast — is the visual antidote to this compression.

**What Happens:** Philip and Abigail are married. Their ceremony took place in a cheap Las Vegas-style chapel full of fake applause and automated confetti. Now they walk through a compressed, fogged, futuristic city toward their car and an unknown destination Phil has been hiding all day. The couple banter, stumble through the city, give away Abby's necklace to a beggar, and escape into the car — their first private moment. As Phil drives, the city gradually thins. Fog gives way to open road. Night falls. And finally: a lake. Sky. Phil reveals a key — their new home. A life beginning.

**What It Means:** This act is the structural inversion of Lucreta. Where Lucreta is stopped at the threshold, Phil and Abby walk through theirs. Where Lucreta's world is dim and controlled, theirs is loud, pressured, alive. Where Lucreta is alone with her condition, Phil and Abby have each other. The act ends with a life beginning — a mirror to Act 01, which ends with a life ending. The connection between the two remains invisible.

## BRIDGE IMAGES — What The Pilot Plants

These images appear in the pilot and will carry forward into later episodes. They are not meant to be explained. They are meant to be felt.

**SKY** — Lucreta reaches for the sky through a window and is pulled away. Abby says they are not privileged enough to see the sky through city fog. The lake scene finally gives both characters — and the audience — sky. Each major Fragment will have a sky moment. Unity will make them connect.

**WINDOW** — Lucreta watches ordinary life through a window she cannot reach. The pilot ends with Abby receiving a life — a new home, a lake, openness — that Lucreta was denied. Later episodes will echo both: a character moving toward a window after surviving what should have ended them.

**EYE COLOR** — Lucreta's pale mint eyes. The teaser's eye color shift. These are introduced as visual details. They will become evidence. The audience does not need to understand them yet.

**RED HAIR** — Lucreta has red hair. This is not incidental. It will bridge to later characters in ways that should feel like recognition when it arrives.

**THRESHOLD / DOOR** — Lucreta collapses before reaching the doorway. Phil and Abby walk through a doorway together at the episode's start. The episode is built around this inversion. Later acts will return to thresholds — crossed and refused — until the pattern becomes undeniable.

**THE CONDITION** — Never named in the pilot. Never explained. Visible only as Lucreta's fading eyes, her cough, her blood, her collapse — and as the brief eye-color shift in the teaser. The audience will form their own explanations. Those explanations will be right, and then they will be too small.

## CHARACTER REFERENCE

**Lucreta**  
Victorian-inspired world. Red hair. Pale mint-green eyes — already fading. Being prepared for a wedding she will never reach. She performs obedience and happiness while her body refuses to express either. She is not depressed. She is trapped in a ceremony that treats her function as more important than her personhood. She collapses before the threshold. She is the pilot's first voice. Her silence teaches the audience how to listen.

**Vera**  
Lucreta's attendant. She cares genuinely — but her care is constrained by ritual. She hides her worry because she refuses to let Lucreta carry it. She is not an obstacle. She is love under ceremony.

**Philip / Phil**  
Futuristic city. The comedy of the episode. He uses humor the way some people use armor — not to hide vulnerability, but to keep the moment moving. He has been carrying a secret all day (the new home) and chosen jokes instead of explanation. He knows Abby well enough to read her silence without making her speak.

**Abigail / Abby**  
Phil's equal and the emotional center of Act 02. Sharp, warm, and unfooled. She feels the city more than Phil does — the compression, the fog, the missing sky, the suffering around her. She gives her necklace to a beggar because it is simply what she does. She cries at the lake not because it is beautiful but because it is open.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

**On Withholding Faces**  
Both acts delay the face reveal. Lucreta is seen from behind before she appears in the mirror. Philip and Abigail are seen from behind, then through the city, before their faces turn. This is intentional. The face should be earned by the world first.

**On Sound Design**  
Act 01 is nearly silent. The city in Act 02 is nearly overwhelming. This contrast is structural. When the car doors close, the drop in ambient noise is a major emotional beat and should be designed as such.

**On the Condition**  
The Condition is never named or explained in the pilot. It appears through Lucreta's eyes, her cough, her blood, and her collapse. It appears briefly in the teaser as a color shift. Resist any impulse to add explanatory dialogue or visual effects. The audience must feel that something is wrong before they understand what it is.

**On the Teaser**  
The teaser requires careful sound design — the music hitting at the exact moment the image resolves is essential to the effect. The letter-dissolve sequence should feel inevitable in retrospect: viewers should be able to re-watch it after several episodes and understand that everything was there from the beginning.

**On Tone**  
The pilot contains genuine warmth and genuine horror and asks neither to announce itself. Phil and Abby are funny. Lucreta is beautiful. The Condition does not care about either of these things. The audience should feel the warmth of Act 02 before they understand that what happened to Lucreta is part of the same story.
